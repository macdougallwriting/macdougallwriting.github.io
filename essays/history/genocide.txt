TRAGIC AND PERVERSE MISUNDERSTANDINGS: WAMPANOAG AND ENGLISH RELATIONS IN SEVENTEENTH CENTURY MASSACHUSETTS


Alexander Macdougall


_______________________________________

Early interactions between European colonizers and Indigenous populations are sometimes painted as misunderstandings. For instance, the naming of the Taino as Indians, and thus the vast overgeneralization and incorrect label 'Indian' being applied to Indigenous peoples across Turtle Island, is a misnomer that egregiously persists to this day. There were few misunderstandings, however, when European colonization of North America began in earnest. The process was well thought out, with a keen implementation of spiritual subversion and physical conquest that was well understood by the colonists. 'Tragic and Perverse Misunderstandings' outlines and illuminates this process within a microcosm of European colonization: early colonial Massachusetts. 

_______________________________________

To say that the genocide of a people, both physically and spiritually, was completed under the pretense of a misunderstanding is to place such events under their most basic and innocent auspices. Instead, the following essay will describe a misunderstanding in the shape of blind ignorance used as a tool for socio-political purposes by European settlers who were largely cognisant of their uncaring, fraudulent, and violently genocidal tendencies against their Indigenous counterparts. Set against the backdrop of English and Indigenous relations during America’s early colonial era, I will explain how racially inspired paranoid delusions alongside a zealous campaign of Indigenous conversion to the Christian faith by Anglo-Puritan settlers would decimate Wampanoag culture via spiritual assimilation, land disruption, and finally war in seventeenth century Massachusetts.
 
The intent to subsume and ultimately destroy Indigenous peoples during England’s early colonial forays in North America can be clearly and immediately defined in the Massachusetts Bay Charter of 1629. King Charles I would proclaim the “principall Ende of this Plantation” to be the inciting of “Natives of Country, to the Knowledg and Obedience of the onlie true God and Savior of Mankinde”,(1) placing this intent to convert Indigenous peoples to Christianity seemingly at the forefront of England’s colonial expedition. The Indigenous peoples of the Massachusetts area seem unintentionally primed for such a conversion, being already acquainted with European language and clothing upon first recorded contact in 1602 near modern Dartmouth,(2) likely a result of prior European contact across the eastern coast of North America spreading via already well-established Indigenous trade networks. Using Martha’s Vineyard as a primary example and supplemented by the existence of racially segregated praying towns, we start to see beyond the misguided spiritual comparisons drawn by Puritan missionaries, opening the gates for the exploration of a further malignancy in cultural misunderstanding wrought by the English in their conflicting ideas on property. A leading figure in the early settlement of the Massachusetts Bay colony, John Winthrop, would note the Indigenous “savages” as holding “no legitimate title… for they inclose no ground, neither have they cattell to maintayne it, but remove their dwellings as they have occasion.”(19) This opposition to the Indigenous semi-nomadic lifestyle would then be ignorantly and arrogantly approached by numerous New England political and religious figures. Hatred for English livestock was also already largely present among the Wampanoag people. Living in close proximity to English settlements - built on the sites of former Indigenous villages depopulated by disease(26) and warfare(27) - led to the wandering of farm animals into Wampanoag cornfields, devastating their crops.(28) Wandering farm animals were also subject to walk into deer traps, with the English holding the local Indigenous liable for their animals injurie’s.(29) Wampanoags found these complaints “hypocritical and hostile” owing to the colonist’s failing to properly fence in their livestock.(30) This heightened tension was made more elevated by ongoing inter-tribal conflicts throughout the mid-1600’s, with representatives of another regional tribe, the Pocumtuck, stating to not have harmed “the colonists or their cattle” while fighting with the Mohegans.(31) The destruction of Indigenous crops by English farm animals, the killing or injuring of said animals by Wampanoag trappers and Indigenous inter-tribal warfare would further entrench a deepening divide in the tenuous Anglo-Wampanoag relationship.

Despite initial successes in the war marked by a violent near expulsion of the Anglo-Puritan colonies back into the Atlantic,  the Wampanoag nation was divided, plundered, and assimilated to the benefit of the English colonists. Such a result was born out of a “cruelty on a massive scale”, with indiscriminate reprisal slayings of Wampanoag men, women, children, and even those innocents who were once loyal to their English counterparts.  The seventeenth century in Massachusetts would end in bloodshed and mark the end of any chance at concluding a peaceful or even benign future between the native Wampanoags and colonial Europeans. Instead, malignant forces would prevail in the forms of spirit, the law, and war. Currently numbered at roughly six thousand, the Wampanoag population only now resembles its size during the English settlement of Massachusetts, and is a far cry from its estimated forty thousand during initial contact in 1602.
