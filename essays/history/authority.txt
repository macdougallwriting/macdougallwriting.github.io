COSMIC CONSERVATISM: LITERARY PROPAGANDA, CENSORSHIP, AND 
EDUCATION IN ELIZABETHAN ENGLAND


Alexander Macdougall

Elizabeth I Tudor reigned over an England in the grips of a cultural boom, particularly in the fields of literature and theatrical performance. Even today, the works of writers such as William Shakespeare and Edmund Spenser retain comfortably long-held positions amongst the curriculums of high school and post-secondary educators across the Anglosphere. However, the English literary expansion during Elizabeth’s reign and the perseverance of contemporary famous works thereafter was in very few ways a form of literary or educational liberalism. Instead, Elizabeth undertook a policy of literary censorship similar to her predecessors though differing by its clandestineness. Concurrent to increasingly more accessible literature – made possible by the printing press and moderately rising literacy rates – and its censorship was the early inklings of a public education beyond private tutors and universities for only the wealthy: the Free Grammar Schools. Like literature of the time, these slow to burgeon institutions propagated more so as tools for social cohesion and stability adherent to the monarchy and parliament than platforms for social mobility. With these notions of censorship and social stability in mind, I argue that Elizabeth I Tudor wished to paint the ongoing literary and educational evolution of England as one befitting her self-reflective image of a stable, peaceful English society. To reach this conclusion, I will analyze propagandic references made by Elizabeth herself and contemporary authors about her rule, the manner of Elizabeth’s punishments and censorships against slanderous literature, and the mild expansion of affordable education that took place during her reign. All things considered, stability was the name of the game and Elizabeth herself wished to publicly embody this stability.

First, I would like to emphasize the perceived cosmic importance of pax terrena – peace in the land – during the reign of Elizabeth and as noted by Elizabeth herself. As historian D.M. Loades states, “[Tudor] Society was a ‘body politic,’ each of whose members existed in a fore-ordained and permanent relationship with the rest…To sow discord in society…was thus not merely a crime but an offence against God.”(1) Elizabeth would note this perceived crucial relationship between the segments of English society herself, vivifying the importance of maintaining such a holy body politic with her unifying humility. Beginning near the end of Elizabeth’s reign, her 1601 “Golden Speech” to Parliament highlights the queen’s political prowess and helps to enforce her image as a ruler whose priorities entailed pax terrena. Made within the context of some growing dissatisfaction in the House of Commons toward business monopolies granted by Elizabeth that resulted in a rising cost of goods, Elizabeth outlined her monopolistic coffers as revenues raised for the good of the people, and described herself as God’s instrument of preservation against “forreigne foes, from Tyrants rule, and from your owne ruine,” a supposed reality made possible by God and her financial benefactors – the very same who were complaining.(2) While shrewd, Elizabeth had presented herself as a humble defender of English society against forces of social disorder. Her success was not just owed to herself and God, but to the very issue at hand and those who seemed to suffer from it – every part of the sociopolitical body had some part to play. Here, in the final days of her rule, Elizabeth framed herself in a manner that she had seemingly desired contemporary authors replicate throughout much of her reign: she ruled with a humility and love for her country that was conducive of peace in the land.

While Elizabeth’s regime kept its largely covert and actively revisionary eyes on publications that may have disturbed the social harmony of England, literacy in general experienced a sustained incline under her reign with the continued growth of Free Grammar Schools, an invention of Edward VI’s regime that continued with neither interference nor assistance by Elizabeth. According to historian David Cressy, the total number of grammar schools during Elizabeth’s rule is difficult to determine, though it is likely that by the Stuart period a majority of market towns and large villages retained at least one grammar school.(11) It seems that Elizabeth and her contemporary Tudor predecessors had few qualms with gradually rising literacy in England, though they also did not contribute greatly to its development. Instead, funding for grammar schools was typically a private affair committed to by local philanthropists.(12) The continued growth of such schools, despite a lack of state funding, is indicative of socioeconomic forces of necessity and want – literacy skills were required for sons who would take over their fathers’ businesses, and lower-class, mostly illiterate families may have wished to see their children climb the social hierarchy. In conclusion, Elizabeth I aspired to be viewed as God’s calm arbiter of social harmony over an England secured by censorship against literary and educational forces of social dissent. Contemporary literature, acts of censorship, and state inaction toward universalizing literacy can help to illustrate this reality. Elizabethan authors and the queen herself made sure to refer to an English queen who espoused character traits befitting of a ruler who placed social harmony and conservation of a stratified social hierarchy as cosmically important. Additionally, actions taken against material deemed in opposition of this image were largely censored in private without much pomp, and only the most direct inquisitions toward Elizabeth’s political rule garnered much in the way of public demonstration and censorship policy adjustments – a relatively even-tempered political strategy that seemingly matched her desire to be illustrated as the peaceful mediator of stability. Finally, despite ruling over England during a time of literary and educational expansion, Elizabeth did little to build upon her predecessor’s introduction of affordable schooling for an initially small number of people. Rather than pursue a policy of liberalized education to further propel the ongoing literary boom, conservative notions of societal cohesion reigned supreme in the form of state inaction.
