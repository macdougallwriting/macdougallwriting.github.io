CASUALTIES OF WORK AND OF WAR


Alexander Macdougall
March 23, 2022

_______________________________________

The following essay was inspired by an accident I witnessed while at work. The industrialization of the world has been an unceasing campaign of production, its casualties recollected in anecdotes and statistics and generally chalked up to as an unfortunate consequence of modernity. The reduction of these casualties across the twenty and twenty-first centuries has been a journey of resistance, regulation, and international industrial reformations that have greatly alleviated industrial accidents across the United States while increasing them in the neo-industrial powerhouses of Asia. 'Casualties of Work and of War' compares the experiences of early industrial workers to those of contemporaneous soldiers to better imagine perspectives around industrial labour in late nineteenth and early twentieth century America.

_______________________________________

“Fell over, outside.” Through his thick Ukrainian accent, I could barely discern what he meant. Maybe a rack of cabinet parts had fallen over? He pulled me by my right arm through the bay doors. What I witnessed next seemed more fitting of a warzone. Luckily for most, life-altering workplace accidents are a rarity. However, during the early twentieth century industrial labor was far more ubiquitous, arduous, and dangerous. American laborers of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries would fight to alleviate themselves from conditions tantamount to those faced by their countrymen in the trenches of Belgium and France. Realizing this equivalency will require a comparison of fatality statistics, an analysis of contemporary American attitudes towards labor and warfare, an exploration of early twentieth-century labor disputes, and an overview of state, federal, and union actions undertaken in the pursuit of an armistice in the workplace. The intention of this paper is to explain the scope and sentiment around early 20th century industrial safety using contemporary and modern sources. Gauging to what degree it was a literal war is up interpretation, but fatalities statistics offer some insight. 

Although direct U.S. military involvement in the Great War spanned slightly less than a year, the total number of combat fatalities was almost certainly deduced with militant precision relative to those calculated by early accident statisticians. With varying assessments, most contemporary statistics between 1906-1915 estimated total yearly fatalities related to workplace injuries in the United States at around thirty thousand.(1) A less conservative tally by the Massachusetts Industrial Accident Board “placed the number of workers… killed by [yearly accidents]”(2) at seventy-five thousand. Associating loss of life and limb during a day’s work to the experiences of soldiers on the battlefield is not a modern purview. In his 1907 magazine article, “Our Industrial Juggernaut,” author Arthur B. Reeve equated the titanic number of dead and maimed American industrial workers to soldiers of the Russo-Japanese War. From his contemporary perspective, “human life [was] cheap – at least cheaper than the cost of protecting the army of toilers from the whirring machinery of the industrial Juggernaut.”(7) While fundamentally decrying the “carelessness of greedy employers,”(8) Reeves also accentuates generalized perceptions surrounding a “disregard for human life.”(9) This notion is enforced by labor advocate and leading suffragette Crystal Eastman, in her 1910 report, “Work-Accidents and the Law.” Then Secretary of the New York State Employers’ Liability Commission, Eastman hints at a sense of normalcy surrounding workplace accidents, claiming “we all know men who would give more happiness by dying than they gave by living.”(10) Her words certainly add an ere of broadly generalized misery to the period, supposing male ‘suicide’ at work to be preferable to a lifetime of destitution and hard labor. While public figures and contemporary writers exposed the miserable and warlike conditions of early industrial wage-labor from the relative comfort of their typewriters, laborers and soldiers alike would produce strikingly analogous works of poetry.

It can be difficult to distinguish between the poetic works of soldiers and workers during the early twentieth century. This is especially true of miners, whose workplace conditions were arguably the most heinous of early American industry.

The language of the above poem, titled “After the Strike” and by an anonymous writer,(11) evokes images of conflict, brotherhood, and righteousness; ultimately ending in a tone of regretful betrayal. The title of this poem makes clear the context for which it was written, though its language and tone seem equally befitting of a shell-shocked soldier failing to follow his comrades into the hellfire of no man’s land. “Strike” may even be inferred in a military sense, as in an artillery strike or movement of troops towards an objective. In turn, a soldier’s poetry can also be seen to inhabit a similar sphere of misery.

Billowing with cold inevitability, “Rendezvous with Death” by Alan Seeger purports a higher degree of hopelessness than “After the Strike.” Even so, a degree of interchangeability exists between the two poems. A “scarred slope of battered hill”(12) may hint at industrial forestry or mining, while workers such as those who Eastman described may well have embraced a “rendezvous with Death.”(13) While both pieces might be artistically analogous, the plainly visible violence of trench warfare is more broadly apparent when comparing these two poems. Seeger perceives to have no choice in the manner of his death, while the anonymous poet-miner at least availed a ‘dishonorable’ escape. Subjective differences aside, the content of both works echo danger, despair, and militancy; words that might also define the nature of the Colorado Labor Wars.

By the 1890’s, notions of conflict involving laborers, business, and the state in Colorado were already well entrenched. Populist Governor Davis H. Waite would exclaim in support of workers, “the war has begun… that eternal warfare of monarchy and monopoly against the right of the people to self-government”.(14) Waite’s claim alluded to the working class’s disaffection towards their employers, then bearing the brunt of the Panic of 1893. During the precipitating 1880’s collapse of silver and prior to the Sherman Silver Purchase Act, Colorado senator Henry M. Teller forewarned Americans of “a system of industrial slavery which will be the worst known to the human race.”(15) Although the demonetization of silver was not the soul force behind America’s ever expanding and immiserating industrial labor caste, Teller’s warning largely came to fruition for a time. Then Governor James Hamilton Peabody stood in martial opposition to strikers led by the Western Federation of Miners (WFM). Declaring “a state of insurrection, and suspended… writ of habeas corpus,”(16) Peabody would call in nearly one thousand soldiers to patrol the region of Teller County, Colorado.(17) The potency of these troops to enforce public order had greatly increased during the period of Reconstruction and beyond (arguably to the present day). Guard units organized out of numerous armories, well armed with the then modern Krag-Jorgenson rifle; alongside more antiquated smooth bore cannons that were “remarkably effective…in riot duty.”(18) Despite high tensions, outright warfare between miners and National Guard units would be restricted to policing actions and dynamite blasting incidents (attributed to members of the WFM).(19) The resulting chaos saw a series of bombings and riots, in which thirteen miners were killed, several hundred deported and “42 turned over to the authorities.”(20) The WFM was defamed to oblivion. The forty-two imprisoned miner’s only option was to renounce their union memberships for freedom. Peabody’s militant reaction to disgruntled Colorado miners seems synonymous to former governor Waite’s supposition of an emboldening class war, all the while concurrent to the fruition of senator Teller’s dystopic industrial wasteland.

Safety mechanisms, the introduction and expansion of regulatory bodies such as the Occupational Safety and Health Administration, and “extensive changes in U.S. economic activity, the U.S. industrial mix, and workforce demographics”  have all played a part in nerfing America’s tragically violent industrial history. While opinions of those like Frederick L. Hoffman regarding the critical nature of industrial activity, relative to the suffering of the maimed, poisoned, and disabled, may seem barbarically ignorant, this notion persists today. The ubiquitous driving of cars, the mass consumption of processed foods, and the overly sedentary nature of modern life may all be perceived to “minister to the absolute needs of the people.”  The war to alleviate late eighteenth and early nineteenth century laborers of their atrocious workplace conditions was championed by individuals who recognized it both literally and figuratively as a state of armed conflict. With similar fatality rates to contemporary conflicts, equivalent poetic attitudes between soldiers and laborers, labor disputes that entailed the use of soldiers as law enforcers, and economic incentives as major drivers for resolution, an uneasy peace would ensue over the battlefields of industry. Although improvements have certainly been made, it should be noted that just as America pursued a series of proxy wars during the mid to late 20th century, so too has it pursued the formation of proxy industries. By exporting most of its manufacturing sector to nations whose own labor laws and conditions are arguably worse than those of early twentieth century America, the issue has simply been relocated rather than solved.
