IS IT WRONG TO CHEAT IN VIDEO GAMES?


Alexander Macdougall
December 8, 2023

_______________________________________

Morality within video games is a topic that often remains confined to their digital, fictional worlds. However, the conversation becomes much more tangible when taking into account competitive video games, particularly during competitions that involve money. 'Is It Wrong to Cheat in Video Games?' argues that cheating in video games is morally wrong, and that the use of something like an exploit or cheat is a similar moral wrong to doping in sports. 

_______________________________________


Somebody stole my spaceship. I could only watch on as another human player waltzed up to my magnificent cosmic cruiser and effortlessly glitched through its shimmering titanium hull. Upon flying off with my ship, my anonymous holo-pirate demanded virtual game credits for the return of my intangible property. A violent frustration roused in me. Was a moral just wrong committed against me, or was I being irrationally attached to an intangible object? Did my digital pirate deserve to be banned from the game like an athlete caught doping? My intuition screamed yes on all fronts: I had been cheated and it felt real. But it was not really real. It was not like my car was stolen, so why was I so upset? Something was going on up in my cranial microprocessor. Perhaps a moral wrong was committed. So, by genesis of emotion, I will attempt to rationally argue that the use of exploits or enhancements in video games to gain an upper hand over other human players is morally wrong; and that such exploits should be dealt with in a manner akin to the policing of doping in sports. First, I will explore how a video game’s illusory experience is only superficially different from physical experiences and interactions. Furthermore, I will explain why the current frameworks which seek to prevent doping in sports should also apply to similar enhancements in video games, and that defying such frameworks ultimately damages the moral self. Walter Glannon’s definition of genetic enhancement will serve as the basis for what this paper considers ‘enhancement’ to be: the upgrading of functions and capacities beyond what is already sufficient (Glannon, p.1).

My first reason stems from the way we experience imagined realities and our own physical reality. It seems necessary to tackle this first, as the most intuitive counterpoint to this entire paper might be that video games are simply light cast on a screen, so any emotional response to cheating is equally fictional. However, this may be an unfair conclusion, as the brain cannot so easily distinguish between reality and illusion. Neuroscientist Don Vaughn states in Keith Stuart’s Guardian article “Medal of Dishonour,” that there is no mechanism in the human brain to differentiate reality from one’s imagination or a game (Keith Stuart, p.3). So, if my brain really thought my spaceship was being stolen, and the perpetrator knew their actions caused some degree of suffering, then their actions would appear to be immoral. Some may argue that this a moot point, akin to someone playing Hitler in a movie and then blaming him for genocide (Matt McCormick, p.278). The issue here is that, although digital, the actions of a gamer who uses exploits to knowingly infringe on other players’ abilities is committed with intention by an individual. So, was it morally wrong for the pirate to steal my ship via a game exploit? My brain sure thinks so. Other brains seem to think so too, as thousands of gamers are routinely banned from multiplayer games for similar exploits (Keith Stuart, p.2). Athletes, too, are routinely banned for cheating by way of performance enhancing drugs. But is this the correct course of action?

The same framework for treating digital enhancement à la biochemical enhancement is already in place: ban the player from playing. Foddy & Savulescu would likely argue against such punishment. Perhaps most relevant to the topic of this paper is the authors’ argument against radical technology as negatively affecting sport. The two counter an argument by philosopher Robert Redeker, who states that leniency toward doping puts a sport on a slippery slope where the athletes no longer embody the spirit of sport. Rather, the athlete has transformed from the best of humanity into an overachieving monstrosity (Foddy & Savulescu, p.517). The two authors go on to claim that equipment advancements, like the gradual modification of tennis racquets, generate their own nearly equivalent modifications of a sport. Though, individuals seem less inclined to equate such progress with that of biochemical advancements, like doping (ibid). I think the issue with their argument is that upgrading equipment and biological enhancement are not as closely related as they may infer. For instance, a runner with the most expensive shoes on the planet may still lose a 100m sprint to a barefoot runner on steroids with twice the muscle strength. If we equalize this scenario one way or the other, it seems to me that the expensive shoes do less to change the athlete than steroids. If we equalize it both ways, then we may have entered an arms race of enhancements where the achievements themselves are praised over the achievers. This, I think, is a reasonable enough position to warrant the maintenance of rules that prevent enhancements or exploits, whether in sports or video games.

Indeed, praising the achievements over the agents of achievement carries its own perceptible immorality, as it may erode individual autonomy and character. Walter Glannon notes that our moral selves and actions derive from our ability to create dispositions that lead to actions (Glannon, p.5). Essentially, my existence as a moral agent hinges on my ability to understand how an act might bring joy or suffering, and to act accordingly. Suppose I know that the 100m sprint is tomorrow and I take a pill that guarantees I will be fifty percent faster than my opponents. Was it really me who won the sprint, or was it the pill? Foddy & Savulescu might again rebuttal by demanding that each athlete is given the same pill. But then all our improved results are bound to the pill and not our disposition to train in an unenhanced manner.  So too with the online gamer. The pirate of my ship did not win his prize through virtual combat; he won it through an oversight in the game engine. His disposition was one of victory by whatever means to the dismay of myself. 

Now I must make the ultimate revelation: I was the pirate all along. I knew full well about this exploit, as it occurred against me once before, and I chose to act upon it to my own selfish interest. Yes, I had achieved victory in some sense, but it was a victory so hollow and devoid of achievement that there was no sense that I had really won anything. It was as if I had teleported from the starting line to the finish line without ever taking part in the race. Like so many activities in life, whether they are recreational or competitive, the knowledge and intent surrounding the activity matters for everyone involved. My brain feels the same way if someone steals my spaceship or if someone takes steroids and whoops me at the track meet, and the rules in either case are there to prevent lopsided defeats, hollow victories, and excessive achievements.
