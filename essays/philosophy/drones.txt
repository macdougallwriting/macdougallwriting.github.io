CUSTOMER SERVICE, I’D LIKE TO RETURN MY DRONE


Alexander Macdougall
November 10, 2023

_______________________________________

Determining blame can be difficult when blame is spread amongst a great number of people. War crimes have been traditionally linked to the regimes and officers who oversaw them; however, autonomous, AI-powered weapon systems have created some confusion as to who to blame if one of these systems 'malfunctions' and commits an atrocity. 'Customer Service, I'd Like to Return my Drone' argues that blame cannot be accurately placed in such situations due to indirect nature of their systems. Specific scenarios and systems may reveal culpability on a case-by-case basis, but the ease of doing so broadly speaking has been hampered by these devices' autonomy.

_______________________________________

The cobbler has been replaced by the factory, the mason by industrial fabrication, the horse-drawn carriage by the car, and so on. Besides furthering the cause of reduced human labor, these advancements have carried with them a plethora of issues related to pollution, wealth division, and ethical quandaries regarding our moral obligations to those around us and those abroad. While automated labor and transportation carry their own interesting moral dilemmas, automated warfare brings further obfuscation to a theater of human activity already beleaguered with a patchwork of attempts at moralization. 

For instance, if an autonomous weapon system (AWS) commits a war crime, then assigning moral responsibility for such an act becomes a tricky pursuit. Perhaps blame could be doled out on a broad scope. If a Canadian drone autonomously flattens a civilian hospital, maybe I, as a Canadian taxpayer, share some ethical responsibility for this action. Just as the relative modicum of CO2 released by my daily activities contributes to anthropogenic climate change, or my mere existence as a player within a global financial system that benefits me to the economic chagrin of so many others, so too does my taxation contribute to the design, development, and deployment of an AWS. While this argument may appeal to some, I do not see an ethical bridge that securely gaps the chasm between the actions of the drone and myself. Much more frightening is that assigning responsibility to the actors most directly linked to the drone is equally difficult. I believe the primary issue here revolves around intentions and, secondly, degrees of knowledge. I argue that indirect actions that carry no ill-intent cannot possibly be the grounds for which moral responsibility can be assigned, whether for climate change, poverty, or psychopathic killer robots. 

Is there any way to place blame fairly and accurately? When framed within an argument like that of individual moral responsibility for human-caused climate change, it seems implausible. Walter Sinnott-Armstrong’s paper “It’s Not My Fault” argues as such. Essentially, his argument boils down to a conclusion that there are no defensible moral principles to argue against gas-guzzling Sunday drives (Sinnott-Armstrong, pp.344-345). I will focus on two of the principles that I believe are most relevant to the discussion at hand: the group principle and the doctrine of double effect. In the former, the author states that we have a moral obligation to not perform an act if it lumps us into a group whose combined actions cause suffering; yet this begs the question that individual acts are bad because the actions of the group are wrong (Sinnott-Armstrong, p.340). So too, does it seem to beg the question that the average citizen has contributed to an AWS war crime because he contributes his tax money as part of a group. He did not purchase the drone, after all, the country did. In the latter, the author states that people have a moral obligation to avoid intentional harm against anyone; yet a Sunday drive is done without the intention to harm (Sinnott-Armstrong, p.295). Similarly, both the taxpayer and the military commander did not intend for a war crime to occur. It seems to follow that both principles fail to direct blame against individuals associated with an AWS that commits a war crime. 

My second major point confronts the problem of physical proximity. Peter Singer notes in his paper on global poverty, “Famine, Affluence, and Morality,” that instant communication and expedient transportation have shifted the moral responsibilities of individuals across the globe (Singer, p.232). I believe that there are some problems regarding this position that are relevant to the moral obligations related to a haywire AWS. For example, there is a distinct difference between direct and indirect knowledge. Regarding the obligation to make donations toward famine relief, the kind of knowledge I can ascertain around how my donated money is used can never be as sound as my direct knowledge of a person suffering in my immediate proximity. I can know through experience that saving a drowning child (Singer, p.231) really did result in the alleviation of suffering. However, I can never truly know the breadth of my contributions to famine relief except perhaps through pictures, video, or text. This is not to feign ignorance, but to say that more uncertainties are present with indirect knowledge. My donations may be subsumed by an armed militia, by corrupt government officials, or lost in a transportation accident. I can only hope that my contributions pan out, but I can never directly know through experience unless I follow my money, the shipment, and the delivery. As with the drone, I cannot have direct knowledge of its actions to link a causal chain of responsibility. Whether my finances are destined for famine relief or war crimes can only be assumed based on a scale of probability. I may intend for the best in either scenario, but indirect knowledge of how my passive actions pan out can never be as secure as direct knowledge and active intent. 

If pinning the faults of an AWS on individuals across a wide berth of society is indeterminate, perhaps the easier route is to place blame amongst those nearest to the crime; those with the most direct knowledge and plausible ill-intent. As the Red Cross alludes to regarding the ethics of operating an AWS, intent may play a direct role in evaluating the outcome of an AI-determined attack (IRC, p.11). Robert Sparrow’s paper “Killer Robots” tackles this issue of intent. Sparrow analyzes whether the programmer, the commander, or the machine itself may be the culprit. The programmer seems innocent, as accidents may be acknowledged, and the drone’s autonomy is encouraged. The commander also seems innocent, as a multitude of weapon systems, autonomous or otherwise, may accidentally fail. Finally, placing blame on the machine itself seems intuitively absurd (Sparrow, p.69-71). Sparrow goes on to relate AWS to child soldiers, insofar as the two ‘weapons’ having similar moral autonomy (Sparrow, p.73). A similar, more lighthearted analogy might involve my theoretical pet dog, Fluffers. One day, Fluffers, who has been nothing but loyal, loving, and adorable, decides to obliterate the kindergarten down the road to the best of his canine ability. This is a tragic surprise for me. Although I am his owner, it seems difficult to place the moral fault on me, given Fluffer’s innocent past. I may be liable under the law, but was it really my fault that Fluffers committed such an atrocity? The same may be said of an AWS, whether we claim that it was the programmer, the commander, the machine, or the society whose combined finances made it possible to field the device. We cannot imprison entropy, after all.   

To unfortunately surmise, the issue of moral responsibility regarding an AWS committing a war crime cannot be determined. The average citizen, even one who might cry out in support of such acts, exists so distally from the act itself to make their burden of guilt essentially non-existent. Even those who we might more intuitively blame, the military commander or the weapon designer, seem separated from any fault by their own agency and that of the weapon. Once upon a time, the cobbler down the road could guarantee that my shoes would fit me; I could blame him if they did not. If my shoes don’t fit me now, I have nobody to blame but the factory in Vietnam operated by the company in Michigan. Who really made my shoes fit so poorly? Who really bombed that school? Is there anybody out there who I can blame?
