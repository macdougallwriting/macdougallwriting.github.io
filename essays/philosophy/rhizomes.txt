THEORETICAL CONCEPT PAPER: PLACE, SELF, MOVEMENT, AND STORY


Alexander Macdougall
November 11, 2025

_______________________________________

Since my first time attending a first-year college philosophy course, I have been fascinated by theories of consciousness. Materialism and dualism typically dominate any argument over what it means to be conscious, though panpsychism presents a third, more fringe theory: everything is conscious. Panexperientialism, a subset of panpsychism, posits that even the most basic of interactions, like two atoms repulsing or attracting one another, are experiences, and to experience constitutes consciousness. I believe that such a position does well to redefine and recontextualize our mental existence from an anthropocentric one to something more universal. Similarly, philosophical theories on place-based inquiry push traditional boundaries of what constitutes a place and define interactions between beings and places as a mutual relationship rather than one of agency and powerlessness. While panpsychism universalizes the mind, place-based inquiry divulges the innumerable connections and resulting affects between and within minds. Recognizing, exploring, and acting upon the infinite multitude of interrelated aspects of our world may be a tremendously affective onto-epistemological practice, particularly within the field of early childhood pedagogy. I argue that place-based inquiry offers two broad categories within which early childhood education might benefit: notions of self and of movement. First, place-based inquiry challenges traditional Western notions of the individualized self and, arguably, presents a notion of self that is more ethically equipped and better matched to the interconnected complexities of reality. Second, place-based inquiry redefines the epistemological aims of education from rigid completionism to malleable progressivism and builds relationships through personal narratives. 

THE SELF AND PLACE

Place-based inquiry presents the self as a world-maker whose world-making has ethical consequences.

Margaret J. Somerville writes in Place Pedagogy Change that places construct who we are through a process of ‘world-making’ (2011, p. 2). World-making demands an initial recognition of self to the other, a focus on engagement between parties, and an anticipation of the unforeseen (2011, p. 2). In this sense, it is both an active and passive approach to being in a place that requires some careful attention paid to one’s surroundings – be they people, light fixtures, tress, or anything. The willingness and capacity to do so, Bronwyn Davies writes, is critical for the ethical health of a community (2014, p. 11). It does not take too much stretching of the imagination to see that dialogues engulfed in relationality and an acceptance of possible unknowns make for more ethical conclusions. A judge who refuses to hear or clearly understand a defendant’s case because he is bored is entirely unethical. Similarly, a teacher who glosses over a child’s story, no matter how strange or unforeseen, may be said to have committed a comparable moral faux pas. Instead, recognizing relationalities at play, engaging with some thought, and expecting the unexpected helps to ethically build spaces and thus relationships. When we communicate, we build the world, and doing so ought to be done ethically.

Place-based inquiry presents the self as more than a subject or object.
 
Iris Duhn argues that viewing places as assemblages breaks apart the traditional view in pedagogy of viewing teachers and students as a subject-object relationship (2012, p. 99). Doing so, she writes, requires shifting attention away from an individual, myopic drive toward competent independence and toward the vast entanglement of forces at play during a child’s education (2012, p. 104). Places are complex networks of forces – organisms, ideas, tech, rivers, emotions – and an educational system that works toward separating individuals from the assemblages of reality by creating entirely empirical observers places humans in an ontological category that insinuates the self as separate from the world. Within classrooms, such individual notions might serve to widen the gap between both teachers’ and students’ unnatural perceptions of one another as authority versus adherent. The reality is certainly much more complicated and deserving of its recognition: there is no hierarchy in the classroom, but there is a complex web of personalities, objects, and space. Recognizing this perspective, in my opinion, helps to form real relationships rather than the standard, clinical teacher-student dichotomy. 

MOVEMENT AND PLACE

Place-based inquiry presents meaning as ongoing and never finalized.

Authors Hamish Ross and Greg Mannion write that building a curriculum is “a process of living in the world rather than one of representing it” (2012, p. 305). The authors examine Tim Ingold’s work on dwelling, which claims that worldly meaning is wrought by active participation in the world rather than by fixed representations (2012). From this perspective, it is not a poem as an individual unit that generates meaning; instead, it is the ongoing writing, reading, and active interpretation of a poem that creates meaning. When a poetry student points toward a theme found within a poem and thinks to herself that her assignment is finished, she is wrong. Even after the acts of exploration, discovery, and synthesis, the theme persists as something affective whether the process was profoundly illuminating or utterly boring – another branch extending the infinite rhizome of life.  Grades, good or bad, are certainly not an accurate representation of our ongoing reality, nor is seeing assignments as totally finalized by satisfying conclusions.

Place based inquiry presents relationships as formed within narratives

Somerville claims that the status of our relationship with a place depends on how we tell stories about it (2011, p. 336). The stories we tell about a place carry with them all our biases, and some caution is likely warranted when we do so. The name Vancouver, for example, carries with it a particular colonial narrative. The story of Vancouver is thus bookended in the past by its own name; its story, as implied merely by its European name, begins with the expulsion of another tale and pays little credence to the area as a place of habitation for time immemorial. Somerville tells us, though, that there can exist a multitude of stories about place existing simultaneously, and that separating local stories rather than hybridizing them may be useful insofar as preserving differences (2011, p. 338). Within an early childhood education context, it seems important to similarly pay due diligence to individual stories. So, when a child tells the story of falling off her bicycle and scraping her knee on the concrete, it is a story that owes attention to a particular individual. And, if we cannot separate ourselves from the world around us, then her story tells us something cosmic, something about a person and place who are one and the same. When she scraped her knee, the universe scraped its knee, and that seems like a worthwhile story. 

In sum, place-based inquiry is an illuminating shift away from standard Western notions of an individualized self and a companion for more ethical communication. Moreover, place-based inquiry better reflects educational demands relative to our reality and provides space for the telling of meaningful individual narratives. Personally, I find the aspects of place-based inquiry that have to do with our being inseparable parts of the world most intriguing and consequential. Like panpsychism’s view of universal consciousness, place-based inquiry offers an alternative to modern onto-epistemological assumptions about our selves and our world. Rather than individual robots programmed to quantize, consume, and compute, we are innumerable vines across a nigh infinite landscape. Our thoughts, actions, and emotions ripple out, continuously affecting and being affected by the world – even when we might think of ourselves boring or inconsequential. There are certainly strong counterarguments to such a position, though I hope to have shown that embracing elements of place-based inquiry has tangible benefits within an early childhood education context regardless of which particular system of knowledge is deemed ‘most correct.’
