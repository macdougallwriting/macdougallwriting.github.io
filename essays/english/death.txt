FACING DEATH IN CHILDREN’S LITERATURE: COMPARING “THE JUNIPER TREE,” “THE LITTLE MERMAID,” AND THE LITTLE PRINCE


Alexander Macdougall


_______________________________________

Although [not so] superficially innocent, fairy tales, lullabies, and children's literature are genres where death is a common, recurring motif. Characters experience death defying odds, martial combat, tragic backgrounds, and death itself throughout many famous pieces of children's literature. 'Facing Death in Children's Literature' analyzes the various ways death motivates and transforms characters across three pieces of children's literature: "The Juniper Tree," "The Little Mermaid," and The Little Prince. 

_______________________________________

Death can be tragic, or it can be a relief. It can be terrifying, gruesome, horrendous, dreadful, and downright depressing. But it can also be guiding, enlightening, perspective altering, unifying, and unbelievably uplifting. The former adjectives are almost certainly associated with the more common perspective of death. I would be hard pressed to recall a time when the passing of a loved one was met with any sort of jovialness. Despite this doom and gloom, children’s literature is adorned with death. It is deeply woven into fairy tales and popular children’s novels with varying degrees of subtlety. Some fairy tales, like the Brother’s Grimm’s “The Juniper Tree,” present death as horrific and macabre. Other fairy tales, Hans Christian Andersen’s “The Little Mermaid” for example, describe death in more ethereal and romantic terms. Finally, some children’s novels, like Antoine de Saint-Exupery’s The Little Prince, postulate death as a transitory experience of the self to distant celestial bodies. While the presentation of death in each of these three pieces of literature varies, they moreover share numerous similarities. Across all three pieces of writing, death empowers the protagonist, narrows their agencies, and is a largely trivial matter that is conquered without confusion or dread. 

It may seem strange to conclude that death might empower a child-character, but this is the case in each of the three above stories. In “The Juniper Tree,” the decapitation of the older brother by his stepmother, though horrifically disturbing, allows the boy to transcend the mortal plane by inhabiting a beautiful songbird (Grimm). Ordained with the ability to produce heavenly music, the songbird’s grizzly and melodic tune wields a mighty power of persuasion over those who hear it. While the young boy in “The Juniper Tree” finds empowerment in the form of a magical songbird, Andersen’s titular mermaid is inspired not just by her infatuation with the prince, but by the somber metaphysical reality of mermaids: utter oblivion after a three-hundred years long life (Andersen, 23). To have prince charming for a time is fine, but an eternal afterlife spent together in blissful serenity is a truly glorious wish for the young aquatic hybrid, especially given her disdain toward the more supposedly resolute demise of a mermaid. Essentially, she is bolstered to pursue a more idyllic death than that of a typical mermaid. Finally, Saint-Exupery’s little prince actively seeks out and makes a bargain with death to guarantee his return home. After traveling the stars and pondering the estranged nuances of adulthood, the prince realizes the monumental affections he has for his one true rose and uses the bite of a venomous snake to propel himself back to his tiny planetoidal home (de Saint-Exupery, 108). He embraces death and uses it as a vehicle to make a reunion with a loved one. Gone are the questions about the dangers of keeping a sheep alongside his rose. His vision focuses on his desired path as he righteously plunges into the unknown, totally emboldened by and assured of the reunion that death propels him toward.

In addition to being empowered by death, each character’s agency becomes much more focused under the specter of death. While he provides his romantically esoteric wisdom regarding his rebirth amongst the stars, the prince’s spirit leaves Earth of its own volition. To explain further, the little prince spends much of his story pondering the wisdom of adults. Eventually, after jumping from planetoid to planetoid with a childlike ambiguity and innocence (de Saint-Exupery, 50), he realizes that his path to happiness lays along a terminal highway that runs back to his love. His prior confusions are put to rest, and he affirms the first step toward self-actualized and blissful contentment at the abandoning of his earthly vessel. The older brother in The Juniper Tree, after his gruesome demise, similarly takes more direct action while under the umbrella of death. As the reincarnated songbird, he repeats a verse that determines his eventual retribution (Grimm). Initially, his character is largely without much agency, he simply exists through the death of his biological mother and his father’s remarriage without much plot interaction. However, after experiencing death himself, the newly apparated raptor has very clearly defined goals: secure the gold chain, the red shoes, and the vengeful milling stone. The little mermaid is similarly refined in her motives by death, as it is a constant reminder that her quest is a timed affair. This is acutely apparent when the mermaid’s sisters offer her a violent reprise: kill the prince and live the rest of your life as a mermaid again (Andersen, 46). Her choice to reject this fate and plunge into the abyss shows a great deal of self-determination and highlights the extremity of her highly ethical behavior. Conclusively, her agency is narrowed down by the zero-sum nature of her magical gambit until her only obtainable decisions are selfish murder or selfless suicide.

What remains so, perhaps unsurprisingly, curious about how death emboldens and focuses each character, is how dying is never a seemingly insurmountable feat for each protagonist.  The boy in “The Juniper Tree” receives zero opportunity to dwell on the matter but, following his death, his direct and effective actions as the songbird lead to a fluid reincarnation into his prior human form (Grimm). He experiences death, accrues fantastical agency as the songbird, and returns to life without too many speedbumps. Quite the experience for a reincarnated child to navigate through so handily. Similarly, the little mermaid, though nagged with the burden of demise, is so selfless in the face of death that she refuses a softened final judgement for self-dissolution (Andersen, 46). The little mermaid is, at the age of sixteen, already a paragon of helpless romance. Without fail, she elects ethical suicide over life-saving murder. Her desires, namely her unfettered adorations for the prince, and her bravery in the face of certain doom make her oddly altruistic for her age. Lastly, the little prince has next to no qualms about dying and reincarnating (de Saint-Exupery, 107), and here lies the shared crux of this matter: by the end of each tale there is a life waiting for each character after death. None of them, aside for the little mermaid’s supposition that she faces true nothingness, actually die, at least in the literal sense of the word. The little prince almost assuredly returns to his stellar abode and seems already well-versed on the transition. Comparably, the boy in “The Juniper Tree” performs multiple reincarnations, while the little mermaid moves into a spiritual limbo with the dangling possibility of a transition into yet another state of existence.

Death is still a bitter event for these characters, but each of them faces it with wisdom, bravery, and action that seems well beyond their years. It empowers them with magical abilities, refines their power to make choices, and is confronted with sublime heroism. Each character has a nearly God-like assurance of a guaranteed life after death, and they tackle the event with supernatural confidence. For their age, they are unlikely exemplars of high morality and wise action. When imagined literally, these traits seem unbelievable. However, within the context of a children’s tale they are less curious anomalies. A dualistic view of the universe, where-by an intangible something exists alongside the matter of reality, is a much more comforting conclusion to life. Perhaps our spirits do move on, into birds, humans, or higher planes of existence. Or perhaps we do face eternal oblivion. No matter the truth, these three stories present death as empowering, focusing, and entirely conquerable. For while we live, it motivates and clarifies our existence and tests the strength of our ethics. It is when we are dead that it might finally become a non-issue - child’s play compared to living.
