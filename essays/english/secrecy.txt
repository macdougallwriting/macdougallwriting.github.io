SWORN SECRECY OR IGNORED HISTORY: A CALL TO UNMASK AND HONOUR CANADA’S CODE TALKERS


Alexander Macdougall
December 3, 2024

_______________________________________

Information and intelligence gathering has perhaps never been as critical to warfare as it is today. The Second World War was particularly formative toward this reality, with [in]famous examples of national secrecy like the Manhattan Project, the construction of the Pentagon, or ENIGMA painting the conflict as one fought behind closed doors as much as it was through them. Indigenous servicemembers were huge contributors to the Allied intelligence effort, translating encrypted messages from English to various Indigenous languages and back again to confuse Axis intelligence gathering units. Although their invaluable efforts have seen some recognition in the post-war and beyond, details around the actions of the code talkers remains scant. 'Sworn Secrecy or Ignored History' examines potential reasons behind the lack of academic literature and general media specific to the experience of Canada's code talkers. 

_______________________________________

During both World Wars, Indigenous military officers from across Turtle Island served as ‘code talkers’ amongst the Western Allies. Many of their languages, then largely undocumented by both allies and enemies, became potent tools for transmitting and receiving critical, undecipherable intelligence data across the battlefields of Europe and Asia. While a fair amount of documentation and media has been generated about Indigenous code talkers of the U.S. armed forces, particularly pertaining to Diné – colloquially known as Navajo – servicemembers, academic literature and popular media specific to Canadian code talkers is surprisingly scant. Bones of Crows, a 2022 Canadian drama, is one recent example of a film whose Indigenous protagonist serves as a code talker while on station in England during the Second World War. Despite critical acclaim, including five Canadian Screen Award nominations and a 2023 television series that expanded upon the protagonist’s family history, there remains little in the way of readily available public information or academic papers regarding Indigenous-Canadian code talkers and their vital contributions to the Allied war effort. 

For the purposes of this paper, I have sought to examine why Canada’s code talkers have remained a largely unexplored and popularly unknown facet of Canadian military history, especially as explanations found in newspaper and encyclopedia articles about this specific issue are particularly unsatisfying. My argument toward explaining such a phenomenon is as follows: the treatment of Indigenous-Canadian soldiers in general and following the Second World War was an overall shameful practice by both the Canadian government and society; as such, the contributions of all Indigenous servicemembers – including the invaluable code talkers – have largely gone unrecognized and underappreciated with only sporadic individual recognitions made as of the 2000s. Due to a lack of primary and secondary documents specific to Indigenous-Canadian code talkers, this paper will have to extrapolate some material regarding Indigenous-Canadian soldiers in general alongside information concerning Indigenous-American code talkers to reach my conclusion. It is likely unsurprising to most readers that racism and social inequality have contributed to this historical blindness given Canada’s colonial past. However, Canada’s slow pace of recovery and historical rediscovery relative to the United States’ makes this a troubling contemporary issue for all Canadians and may be yet another indicator of our country’s persistently heinous outlook toward its Indigenous peoples.

First, I would like to debunk one seemingly common explanation for why the code talkers remain a ‘covert’ topic of interest insofar as readily available academic research and common public knowledge is concerned. According to TheCanadianEncyclopedia.ca, much of the post-war occlusion about code talkers is partly to do with their sworn secrecy (Scott, par. 1). Indeed, former Canadian code talker Charles “Checker” Tompkins acknowledged this reality, swearing to secrecy until the final days of his life when he finally spoke about his experience as a code talker (Lazarowich). The continued secrecy of the code talkers themselves in the post-war certainly speaks to their personal adherence to duty and honour, though it does not tell the whole story. The problem here, I believe, is that the overall secrecy of the code talkers’ operational existence was already somewhat broken before the Second World War and was later officially disclosed nearly forty years prior to Tompkins’ own revelations. 

Prior to World War II, Indigenous-American code talkers had already been publicly acknowledged in 1930s newspapers following their similar intelligence contributions during the First World War (Walker, 94). Moreover, German intelligence was acutely aware of the code talkers, sending spies who posed as anthropologists to study Indigenous languages across the United States in the years prior to World War II. Diné was one of the few language groups in the U.S. that went unobserved by German ‘anthropologists,’ so it was elected as one of the primary code talker languages for the U.S. Marines (Walker, 97). Without a source to confirm, I can only postulate that Cree and its many dialects was elected as one of the primary code talker languages by the Canadian military due to a similar lack of Axis linguistic knowledge about Cree. Other plausible reasons include Cree soldiers’ familiarity with both English and French, a large population of speakers with varied dialects from which to draw upon, and a desire to supplement languages, like Diné, already employed by the United States (Scott, par. 8).  Finally, and perhaps most pertinently, the code talkers have been declassified in Canada since 1963 (Crawford, par. 10). So, while it certainly makes sense for total sworn secrecy to be upheld during and perhaps immediately following WWII and as a way for Indigenous officers to individually uphold the honour of their sworn secrecy thereafter, it seems strange that little research specific to Indigenous-Canadian code talkers has been done nor has further information been made more easily accessible decades after the war’s end and the program’s declassification. As The Canadian Encyclopedia notes without elaborating upon much further, sworn secrecy was only ‘partly’ to do with the still covert code talkers (Scott, par. 1). So what other aspects are ‘partly’ to do with this persisting secrecy?

As stated in my thesis, some of the persisting ‘secrecy’ around the code talkers is likely to do with the overall poor treatment of Indigenous-Canadian soldiers following the conclusion of the Second World War, leaving these soldiers with a still underappreciated and largely unknown legacy. Firstly, returning Indigenous veterans found employment in post-war Canada difficult due to a reduction in wartime industries and overt racism. Indeed, most returning veterans experienced a dramatic drop in income as the war industry decreased overall domestic production (Innes, 692). Moreover, Indigenous soldiers faced the compounding issue of racism when trying to find post-war employment. One Indigenous veteran said regarding this issue that “They just regard you as nothing. And that’s how we were treated…When we were discharged they told us that we would get first choice of any job at all. But that was impossible” (Innes, 694). Clearly their service did little to change the contemporary outlook of Canadian employers. It is unfortunate that, whether for practical or honourable reasons, the significance of the code talkers was not more well known at the time. Though, pondering about whether knowledge of such would have contributed to meaningfully changing contemporary white Canadians’ racial biases is the sort of conjecture that I am generally pessimistic about, especially when considering the apparent continued disinterest in the topic following its 1963 declassification. 

Secondly, many Indigenous-Canadian veterans faced alienation in their own communities and were embittered by the still oppressive white dominated society they were returning to. In reference to lands given by the Veterans’ Lands Act – to both Indigenous and non-Indigenous veterans – and following the Second World War’s conclusion, Gordon Ahenakew said that “[Community members] didn’t really understand ‘cause I got land. I got my 35 acres and they didn’t have anything. They were jealous of that…They just didn’t understand” (Innes, 696). Perhaps such misunderstandings were due to confusions or objections over fighting a white man’s war and being awarded something in exchange that so many Indigenous peoples had been wrongly bereft of by the very same white society: land. Whatever the case, some Indigenous veterans who did receive compensation from their government went on to face difficulties and misunderstandings from their own communities as a result thereof. Additionally, coming back to life on a reservation presented a sort of morbid irony for many returning veterans, as they had just returned from a war to liberate Europeans from their repression under Nazi rule only to return to their own marginalization in Canada. As one Indigenous veteran put it, “it’s almost you know funny how a freedom loving people…closed [us] in here” (Innes, 697). The pot calling the kettle black comes to mind. This kind of two-facedness toward social freedom is not a distinctly Canadian phenomenon, though I believe that it is an important reality to bear in mind when considering why the Canadian code talkers and Indigenous contributions to the Allied war effort in general have remained popularly unacknowledged in Canada. Perhaps the experiences of Canadian liberators returning to their oppressive motherland weaves an unappealing narrative given the context of post-war victory over the tyranny of the Axis. It frames the ‘good guys’ in an unattractive and ambiguous moral way, not the sort of image a victorious nation would like to see of itself – conceivably even decades after the war. 

For the last portion of this essay, I would like to highlight modern progress toward acknowledging the contributions made by the code talkers and how Canada has been less proactive about such conclusions relative to our southern neighbours. In 2008, then President George W. Bush signed the Code Talkers Recognition Act to recognize and honour the contributions made by all Indigenous-American code talkers who served in both World War I and World War II (Boileau, par. 10). Comparatively, Canada has done little to officially recognize the overall efforts of the code talkers. In 2018, code talker and Mohawk warrior Louis Levi Oakes was recognized in Ottawa for his wartime contributions by the Assembly of First Nations, the Speaker of the House of Commons, and current Prime Minister Justin Trudeau (Boileau, par. 12). While recognizing Oakes’ invaluable service to his country is a positive gesture, neither the offices of former Prime Minister Stephen Harper nor current Prime Minister Justin Trudeau have yielded a similarly comprehensive code talker recognition like their counterpart in Washington. Despite performing the same critical intelligence duties as Indigenous-American code talkers, the Indigenous code talkers of Canada remain officially unrecognized in totality by the government after seventy-nine years of post-World War II history, sixty-one years of declassification, and sixteen years since official U.S. honours were signed. Official recognitions made by the Government of Canada may help alleviate our country from an overarching ignorance toward a piece of significant Canadian military history that has persisted in unwarranted secrecy for too long.

Canada has had a history of struggling with its history, and the code talkers are another example of a historical facet left unacknowledged and broadly unhonoured. Allied intelligence was a massive contributor to the overall victory over the Axis powers, and the undecipherable languages of Indigenous nations from across Turtle Island played a key role in keeping Axis signals interceptors in the dark. Despite this, Indigenous code talkers returned to a homeland where racism infringed upon employment, alienation arose in their own communities, and their contributions were left mostly unknown in a kind of faux secrecy decades after the war. While I can appreciate the continued secrecy of their individual work as being partly attributed to personal honours, I fail to see how the intelligence group’s persisting secrecy serves any practical purpose – especially given the amount of readily available information on arguably more clandestine World War II operations like the Manhattan Project and the Enigma machine. If anything, maintaining the secrecy of the code talkers has seemingly meant that their crucial involvement has gone unnoticed to the point of being detrimental to honouring their memory. Call me treasonous, but I would prefer to see the code talkers of Canada uncovered before they fall further into historical obscurity.
