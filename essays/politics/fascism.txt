FEELING FASCISM: HOW MODERN EMOTIONAL APPEALS INDICATE THE FASCISM OF TRUMP AND TRUMPISM


Alexander Macdougall
December 2, 2025

Fascism is perhaps one of politics’ most nebulous and emotive terms. Experts are divided as to what fascism is exactly and who, historically and presently, fascists are. Some popularly shared commonalities do exist; though, as author Joachim Scholtyseck writes, there are few enough similarities to accurately describe something of a generic fascism.(1) Despite this difficulty, fascism is popularly thought of as being authoritarian, ultranationalist, racist and/or xenophobic, warmongering, and mythologically driven. Additionally, many writers on fascism, whether liberal or conservative with their definitions of fascism, would generally agree that Fascist Italy and Nazi Germany are exemplars of fascist states. What can be said, then, about the current administration in Washington D.C.? Experts and pundits alike decry President Donald Trump as a fascist, while others urge caution in jumping to inaccurate or emotive appeals when paralleling Trump to Benito Mussolini or Adolf Hitler. As Samuel Moyn says, “Without acknowledging differences, comparison is partisanship, and not always in a good cause.”(2) With Moyn’s point in mind, I hope to prove that comparisons of emotive appeals made by fascist leaders are a critical component to defining the fascism of Trump and Trumpism, while also bearing in mind the different contexts and manifestations of present-day fascism. I believe that enough has been said insofar as comparing events like January 6th, aesthetics like red hats, mythological throwbacks, and Trump’s general disdain for American politics to the actions, aesthetics, mythos, and anti-democracy of figures like Hitler or Mussolini. Instead, I advocate for emotive political appeals against the marginalized ‘other’ by Trump as a telltale criterion of fascism that deserves greater attention. Fascist leaders have regularly espoused emotive appeals against ‘the other’ as a driving force for the implementation of authoritarian policies. Richard J. Evans writes that the drive for fascism in Nazi Germany was the desire to refight the First World War and win it, a militant political drive that appears absent or at least relatively subdued within the Trump administration.(4) What is perhaps lost in this analysis, however, is the more general animating drive shared between Mussolini, Hitler, and Trump’s initial political successes: the creation of ‘the other’ and a general narrative of national loss rather than one specific to military defeat. While Mussolini and Hitler may have had more concrete examples for political appeal (a lackluster victory in the case of Italy; unfair treatment by the Treaty of Versailles, forced disarmament, territorial loss, and economic strife in the case of Germany), Trump’s blaming of contemporary issues in the United States on unfair trade policies and immigrants painted as villainous caricatures has formed an emotive petition amongst voters not unlike those attracted to the antisemitism and ultranationalism of Hitler. According to a poll by ABC News, Republican voters in the 2024 election preferred Trump over Kamala Harris on issues of the economy and immigration.(5) I think it is important to consider the fact that voters determined Trump to be the stronger candidate within these categories after he had blamed immigrants for eating peoples’ pets and painted American allies as at best opportunistic and at worst parasitic. Although blame is a routine device of politicians, it is the degree to which blame made by fascist politicians obfuscates truth and incites hatred that separates the initial modus operandi of a fascist from a conservative or neoliberal politician. Hannah Arendt wrote in 1945 that fascists had “exploited the age-old [Western] prejudice which confuses reality with truth,” elaborating further that Nazi Germany had essentially sewn chaos and violence to prove chaos and violence.(6) Similarly, when Trump raised National Guard forces to fight crime in Washington D.C., among other cities, he capitalized on the shooting of two guardsmen by an Afghan immigrant to verbally attack “hundreds of thousands of Somalians…ripping off [the] country.”(7) Like Soviet troops at the gates of Berlin ‘proving’ the threat of Bolshevism, pursuing highly contentious policies like the use of military forces in policing duties is unsurprisingly bound to incite a range of responses, both non-violent and violent. While the sight of soldiers policing cities is already an aesthetic of fascism, it is Trump’s painting of an individual event as the animus to demonize entire ethnic groups that again proves the administration’s fascist appeals. It is not necessarily the shooting nor the lies themselves; rather, it is the intended emotional response incited by the lies that indicates fascism.

Alongside a generally increasing loneliness, more fundamental societal changes ongoing since the late twentieth century mean a fascism that has itself shifted opportunistically – though its emotive appeals remain largely the same. Gille Deleuze, who builds atop the work of Michel Foucault regarding discipline and control societies, notes a shift from a world concerned with institutional discipline (passing in dignified order from one enclosed space to another, from school, to factory, to hospital, to prison) to one of corporate control, whereby “one is never finished with anything – the corporation, the educational system, the armed services being metastable states coexisting in one and the same modulation.”(13) Put simply, the world has drastically changed since the fascist regimes of the early twentieth century. Many of the institutions of the old discipline societies remain, though the ontology of livelihood in the West continues to seemingly drift toward perpetual states of being within themselves, unceasing work within work – the restaurant employee who markets himself on social media while selling products on Amazon during his smoke break. It should be no wonder, then, that fascism should present itself differently in societies of control than it would in societies of discipline. Indeed, much of Trump’s success in both 2016 and 2024 has been at least partially owed to his embracing of the control society through its never-ending meta media – the president who markets himself on Truth Social while selling cryptocurrency during his golf break.

According to Eco, fascism of the twentieth century was “a fuzzy totalitarianism…a beehive of contradictions…monarchy with revolution…absolute state control with free market[.]”(17) Perhaps, then, the clearest demarcation of the Trump regime’s fascism is the tremendous amount of disagreement as to whether it is in fact fascist. The similarities are such that the use of the word fascism within American politics has gone beyond synecdoche and become a proper concern, one apparently worthy of ongoing academic debate. Just as fascist leaders obfuscate truth and reality, the muddy comparability and lack of broad academic consensus regarding Trump and Trumpism is itself worrying. 

I would like to conclude with a short anecdote. My maternal grandparents lived their childhoods in the Netherlands under Nazi occupation. They told stories about the anxieties of an oncoming war, the fear wrought by Hitler’s vitriol cast over the radio waves, and the gradual disappearing of immigrants and Jews. When my grandfather witnessed Trump’s first election, he told me that his decision to relocate to the supposed haven of Canada might not have meant the peace and security he had hoped would last our lifetimes. I believe that fascism has a particular feeling, one that is especially noticeable by those who have witnessed its culmination firsthand. While a recognizable aversion for some, the emotions of hateful blame are for others a seemingly just, simple, unifying pathos – “the demonic force latent in the lives of all the good little people.”(18) Moreover, no two fascists are alike, a fact that I believe is particularly worthy of consideration when trying to pinpoint fascism generally and the fascism of the Trump and Trumpism specifically. As a final comment, the most likely smoking gun insofar as defining Trump and Trumpism as nigh inarguably fascist has yet to occur: a complete toppling of American democracy. MAGA hats sold with text reading ‘Trump 2028’ are certainly foreboding signs, but they are no assurance. If it has not yet been made clear by this essay, though, I believe fascism is fuzzy enough to omit such a criterion. Trump, in my mind, is an incompetent fascist, restrained from loftier totalitarian aims by institutions that, though perhaps weakened, remain buttresses against complete political domination. What makes Trump and Trumpism fascist are the emotive appeals whose flavours are eerily reminiscent to the panacea of deceit and hate found in twentieth-century fascism. Despite a plausible electoral defeat in 2028, Trump will remain a fascist – even if a failed one.
